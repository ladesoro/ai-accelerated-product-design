How AI Helped Build a Custom Scheduling Tool in 5 Months Instead of 12

A behind-the-scenes look at how a product team used AI to accelerate every phase of product development—from first concept to production.

AI didn’t build this. People did. AI simply helped people move at the speed of their ideas.
↓

Act 1

The Breaking Point

In addition to a packaged mobile app, the product team was using a field service desktop scheduling tool from a vendor. It was designed to coordinate scheduling and assignments for field teams across the country. Unfortunately, it didn’t meet the needs of the field teams.

😟

Clunky UX

The interface was slow, unintuitive, and frustrating. Simple tasks took too many clicks, and every interaction felt like a battle against the software rather than a productivity tool.

🔒

Inflexible

Couldn’t adapt to the different working styles and operating models across the diverse field organization. Every team works differently.

⚠️

LMR Paused

This tool was one of the biggest reasons the Limited Market Release had to be paused. It was essentially unusable.

💡 The Turning Point

The question shifted from “How do we configure or fix this packaged app?” to something bolder: “What if we built our own?” A custom scheduling and assignment console—purpose-designed for field teams—built as a UI layer on top of Salesforce data. Going rogue was risky. But doing nothing was worse.

Act 2

One Afternoon That Changed Everything

On February 2nd, the UX Designer opened Figma Make and wrote a single prompt. What happened next would set the trajectory for the entire project.

“I want to create a scheduling tool to help cardiology medical device sales teams add and assign cases for the accounts in their region. There should be a short form to intake the basic case information (account, date, time, type, optional notes). Users should be able to see all cases across all accounts easily, both assigned and unassigned. There should be a way to automate scheduling based on rules the user sets up—like assigning a primary rep to each account. When the user generates an automated schedule with the input cases, they should be able to override and drag/drop cases around to different team members if desired. They should be able to view the schedule grouped by rep or by account. I want to be able to see all assignments in a zero-click, zero-scroll view. Some teams may have 20-30 clinical reps in their region.”

The first output was shockingly good, but wasn't exactly what the designer had in mind. Over the next ~2 hours, she iterated relentlessly via chat. She experimented with an unassigned pool, kanban views, card-based layouts, and calendar views. Each prompt refined the experience, each iteration brought the vision closer to reality. Ultimately, she landed on matrix grid week views organized by week, rep, and account, a format that matched how field teams actually operate and plan. The result wasn't just a mockup... it was a fully coded, interactive React web application.

Core Design Principle

🎯 “Flexibility”

One question guided the design from the beginning: Are we building enough flexibility into this tool so that field teams can make this tool work for them? No two teams operate the same way and working styles vary by account and leadership approach. The tool needed to support that reality, not fight against it.

4:00 PM — Monday, Feb 2

Sent to the Tech Lead

Once satisfied, the Designer sent the prototype to the Tech Lead. He immediately saw the potential. Using Teams, they exchanged ideas, explored possibilities, and imaged what the tool could become.

10:00 AM — Tuesday, Feb 3

POC Coded

Overnight, the Tech Lead took the Figma prototype and hard coded a working proof of concept. In less than 24 hours, the concept transformed into a coded POC.

All Day — Wednesday, Feb 4

Skepticisim gives way to Momentum

During an in-office day, the Designer and the Tech Lead walked the core product team through the Figma prototype. The reaction as immediate, they were floored. After hearing about the POC, the energy shifted from “should we?” to “how fast can we?” What had started as a bold idea suddenly felt inevitable.

That Same Afternoon — Feb 4

Live MVP Session with the Business

The core product team help a working session with the Business PO to define the MVP. The Designer created a branch of the design and used Figma Make to modify features in real time as decisions were made together. By end of session, the MVP was defined and the prototype updated to match.

2 hours of human expertise + minutes of AI execution = months of work eliminated

What would have taken 3–4 months of discovery, research, design concepts, wireframes, design system creation, and prototyping was done in an afternoon.

Act 3

Validating at the Speed of Iteration

The AI-assisted prototype was so real that usability testing felt like testing a live product—not a concept. AI accelerated the research process as well.

Using Claude within Figma Make—the same chat session that generated the prototype—the UX Designer designed the entire usability study. She was extremely prescriptive, iterating and refining every detail. AI kept up with her expertise. Here’s what it helped produce:

  • Usability study structure and methodology
  • Facilitator guide and moderator script
  • Realistic scheduling scenarios and task flows
  • Follow-up survey for qualitative feedback
  • Post-session synthesis from transcripts and observer notes

Act 4

Building at Speed

With a validated design, clear user stories, and a team moving in lockstep, development began on March 16. AI continued to accelerate the work by shortening feedback loops, streamlining development, and helping the team maintain momentum as the product took shape.

The entire end-to-end solution — UI and business logic — built entirely by Salesforce developers.

Without AI, this highly UI-intensive build would have required a dedicated UI developer alongside the Salesforce team. AI eliminated that need entirely — every developer could own the full stack.
